City Latitude & Cloudiness


Have you ever thought about *how* big weather systems move across the land? In the northern hemisphere, for example, they normally move west to east. But bad thunderstorms in the summer don't just hit Texas. They often stretch far north into Missouri and even Minnesota as the whole system moves to the east coast. Fascinatingly, that story I just told about how storms move comes out in the figure to the left!

If a storm is raging in Houston at 30 degrees North, we might expect distant clouds from the same weather system to be affecting the cloudiness in Kansas City, at 40 degrees North. And everywhere in between is probably cloudy for that matter too! So what should we expect to see in terms of latitude and cloudiness? Well, we should expect to see that if it's cloudy in the north it'll be so in the south. That's precisely what the 'lines' we see in the figure are telling us! Cloudiness can be pretty constant across all latitudes. But then why do we see multiple lines? Think about the variable of time! With one visualization, we can use imagery to tell us a story about how storms move across the United States!
